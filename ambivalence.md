#  ambivalence

_Ambivalent_ is a strange word and for a long time I confused it with _apathy_ or _indifference_. I've been thinking about ambivalence a lot lately while collecting writing and thinking about generative AI. In fact, I've been meaning to write this newsletter for months now but every time I thought I had something to think through regarding AI something else would come along and I'd hold off. The notes, ideas and links I've been collecting currently look like this. 

![[ambivalence-notes.png]]

Actually, that's just the first quarter. 

What I was – and remain sure about – is that I have never felt such strong ambivalence towards a technology. I am both enthralled and repulsed. 

Karen Hao's account of OpenAI (which developed chatGPT) in _Empire of AI_ is terrifying on many levels: the environmental and cultural destruction, how we seem to be under the spell of the (mostly) young white men with big technocratic brains and minimal social intelligence, and the sense that those same people running these massive businesses are just making it up as they are going along. Yoshua Bengio – known as the Godfather of AI – said that "[a sandwich has more regulation than AI](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qe9QSCF-d88)".

Note to self: it's probably better to be the Grandfather of something than the Godfather.

Then there Claude's Cowork which is as close to having a technological sous chef as one could imagine. Like HAL9000 except happy to help you do whatever you want without showing any signs of being afraid. It is so much more than a chatbot and exercises my imagination in ways that I find both surprising and joyful.

Yet the sense of fatigue and overwhelm I think I feel from the boom and doom onslaught of AI related news, politics and possibilities is symptomatic of using LLMs themselves. It's that feeling you get when so much text is being generated that your connection with the substance and language itself starts to dissolve. Is it when you slide into the sensation that I am no longer part of these words.

L. M. Sarcasas is a tech writer I read avidly and here he is [thinking through the ideas of Swiss philosopher Max Picard](https://theconvivialsociety.substack.com/p/the-thing-that-is-silence):

> But [Picard] immediately warns that “language becomes emaciated if it loses its connection with silence.” Thus Picard believes, writing in the mid-20th century, that the task before us “is to uncover the world of silence so obscured today—not for the sake of silence but for the sake of language.”

I think this is the embodied aspect of the AI "revolution" that is most crushing: the noise of it all. It is deafening but even writing these few words now as I try to make sense of something is a quiet salve. Perhaps though revolutions were never meant to be quiet. 

The philosopher of Science Isabelle Stengers [wrote that the question that worried Alfred North Whitehead](https://archive.org/stream/IsabelleStengersThinkingWithWhiteheadForewordIntroduction/Isabelle+Stengers+-+Thinking+With+Whitehead+%28Foreword+%26+Introduction%29_djvu.txt) was "that of abstraction and, more precisely, the lack of resistance characteristic of the modern epoch to the intolerant rule of abstractions that declare everything that escapes them frivolous, insignificant, or sentimental". Along with the noise perhaps this is where the AI maelstrom is most worrying or even dangerous for those of us invested in what bodies understand. That the materiality of the body is under threat from the abstractions of what is merely pattern recognition.
