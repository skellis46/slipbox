# a shrinking body

In an [age of technofeudalism](https://www.yanisvaroufakis.eu/2024/04/22/welcome-to-the-age-of-technofeudalism-interviewed-by-wired-magazine/) now seized by the boom or doom hoopla of generative AI, I have long felt safe. 

I have felt safe because my work and artistic practice has been to centre corporeal experiences and sensations that are fundamental to the body in motion. This is the body that I once described as being radically analogue, as if that status of not being binary in the digital sense protected it from what the technology skeptic Neil Postman called the technopoly: 

> the submission of all forms of cultural life to the sovereignty of technique and technology. [@postman-1993-technopoly p.52] 

And I have continued to make the same argument. That is, there are some things that are untouchable regardless of how much money or how much technology is thrown at them. That the core of human experience as felt through the body is inherently resistant to datafication. Even perhaps that somatic and body-based practices like dance have a role to play in challenging the rise of the machine.

But I think I might be wrong.

Towards the end of the essay _Up from Darkness: A Possible Future of Humanism_ the American Professor of Humanities Alan Jacobs discusses Leif Weatherby's book _Language Machines_ and what Weatherby calls "remainder humanism". Jacobs notes that Weatherby's argument structurally mirrors that of Dietrich Bonhoeffer more than 80 years ago: 

> [Bonhoeffer] warned Christians against attempting to defend their faith by pointing to what science cannot explain. This is to make God not the Lord of All but, rather, the Lord only of the “gaps” in our knowledge, of what cannot currently be explained. [@jacobs-2026-darkness n.pag.]

This is the mistake I have been making. 

I have continued to define an ever shrinking embodiment through the hegemonic technopoly. For example, a radically analogue body is explicitly that which is not digital. This is 'remainder corporeality' expressed through the tropes of digital technologies that impoverish bodies being known through motion. The shrinkage is unsustainable as the digital continues to bend, morph and grow, and to encroach on what being bodies might mean.

The answer instead is to express these same bodies not through the lens of generative AI or whatever is next in the technopoly's over-resourced arsenal, but in its own presences, understandings and languages. If we in dance and somatic practices want to sit at any table labelled for example _human centred AI_, then we must bring to that same table language and understanding that is not expressed through ordinal and digital logics. That to be in those conversations will demand that all of the ineffable mangle of being bodies is heard in its own terms.
